Simple Forecasting Techniques for Small Business

You don't need a PhD to predict sales. Here are three easy methods to plan better.

Data & Intelligence April 25, 2026 5 min read

Forecasting feels like a superpower reserved for big corporations with armies of data scientists. But here's the truth: small businesses can predict their future with surprising accuracy using nothing more than a spreadsheet and common sense.

According to a study by the Small Business Administration, only 40% of small businesses use any formal forecasting method. The rest fly blind – ordering too much inventory, hiring too late, or missing growth opportunities. The good news? You don't need complex algorithms. These three techniques work with the data you already have.

Why Bother Forecasting?

If you've ever asked yourself any of these questions, you need a forecast:

  • "How much inventory should I order next month?"
  • "Do I need to hire another person, or can we wait?"
  • "Will I have enough cash to cover expenses in Q4?"
  • "Is this new marketing campaign actually working?"

📊 The bottom line: Forecasting doesn't have to be perfect. It just has to be better than guessing – which is what most small businesses are doing right now.

Method 1: The Historical Average (The "What Happened Last Year?" Method)

This is the simplest forecasting method – perfect for businesses with at least 12 months of sales history.

How it works:

Step 1: Take your sales from the same month last year.
Step 2: Adjust for any known changes (price increases, new competitors, market trends).
Step 3: That's your forecast.

Example: You sold $25,000 in March last year. You've since raised prices by 10% and added a new marketing channel. Your March forecast = $25,000 × 1.10 × 1.05 = $28,875.

✅ Best for: Established businesses with seasonal patterns and at least one year of data.
⚠️ Limitation: Assumes the past predicts the future – doesn't work well during rapid growth or market shifts.

Method 2: The Sales Pipeline Forecast (For B2B & Service Businesses)

If you have a sales team with a CRM or even a simple spreadsheet of leads, this method is for you.

How it works:

Step 1: List every active opportunity in your pipeline.
Step 2: Assign a probability of closing (e.g., 90% for verbal commitment, 50% for proposal sent, 20% for initial meeting).
Step 3: Multiply each deal value by its probability, then sum them up.

Example:
• Deal A: $10,000 × 80% = $8,000
• Deal B: $25,000 × 40% = $10,000
• Deal C: $5,000 × 100% = $5,000
Total forecast = $23,000

✅ Best for: B2B, agencies, consultants, and any business with a defined sales process.
⚠️ Limitation: Only as good as your sales team's honesty about probabilities. Build a culture of accurate tracking.

🎯 Pro tip: Track your win rates by stage over time. If historically 50% of "proposal sent" deals close, use that real number, not a guess.

Method 3: Moving Average (The "Smooth Out the Noise" Method)

When your sales bounce around – up one month, down the next – a moving average helps you see the real trend beneath the volatility.

How it works (3-month moving average):

Step 1: Add the last 3 months of sales.
Step 2: Divide by 3.
Step 3: That's your forecast for next month.

Example:
January: $20,000 | February: $25,000 | March: $18,000
Total = $63,000 ÷ 3 = $21,000 forecast for April

✅ Best for: Businesses with high monthly volatility or less than 12 months of history.
⚠️ Limitation: Reacts slowly to sudden changes (like a huge new client or a market crash).

Which Method Should You Use?

Your Business Type Best Method Why
Retail / E-commerce (1+ year history) Historical Average Seasonal patterns repeat year over year
B2B / Agency / Consulting Sales Pipeline Deals take time to close – pipeline visibility is key
New business (<12 months old) Moving Average Smooths out startup volatility
High‑growth / rapid scaling Combination (Pipeline + Moving Average) Neither method alone is accurate during hockey‑stick growth
📈 Real-world example: The Bakery That Stopped Wasting Dough

A small bakery used to bake the same amount every day – wasting 20% of their products. We implemented a 7-day moving average forecast based on daily sales. They adjusted production daily and cut waste by 65% in one month, saving over $1,200 monthly.

Making Your Forecast Actionable: The 3-Step Review Process

A forecast sitting in a spreadsheet is useless. Here's how to turn it into action:

📅 Step 1: Review weekly (30 minutes)

Compare actual sales to forecast. Calculate variance = (Actual - Forecast) / Forecast. If variance > 15%, investigate why.

📝 Step 2: Document assumptions

Write down what you assumed (e.g., "No major competitors entering market," "Marketing spend remains constant"). When assumptions change, update your forecast.

🔁 Step 3: Update rolling 90-day forecast

Don't forecast a year ahead – it'll be wrong. Maintain a rolling 90-day forecast, updating it monthly with actual results.

Conclusion: Start Simple, Then Improve

The best forecast is the one you actually use. Start with the simplest method that fits your business – even if it's just "last month plus 5%." As you get comfortable, layer in more sophistication: adjust for seasonality, add leading indicators, or use a weighted pipeline.

🔑 The bottom line: A simple, consistent forecast beats a perfect forecast that you never update. Start today with whatever data you have – even if it's just your gut plus a spreadsheet.
